Running a car in Paris is a choice, not a necessity — public transport is excellent and parking is genuinely difficult. But many staff posted to France, especially those with families or duty outside the city, do keep a vehicle, and the diplomatic registration and tax position is different from the ordinary process. This guide explains the shape of it. Specific eligibility, thresholds and time limits change and depend on your status, so each is flagged here for confirmation rather than stated as fact.

## First decision: import, buy locally, or go without

- **Import** a vehicle you already own. This can interact with customs and tax relief available to accredited staff — but relief is conditional and time-limited. *[Verify current conditions and any window with French customs / your mission.]*
- **Buy locally**, new or used, sometimes under a tax-relieved arrangement for eligible diplomatic staff. *[Confirm eligibility and the mechanism before purchase.]*
- **Go without.** For many central-Paris postings this is the rational choice.

## Diplomatic registration and plates

France issues special registration to eligible diplomatic and consular vehicles, distinct from ordinary plates. The categories and what they signify are set by the authorities; your mission registers the vehicle rather than you doing it at a standard préfecture counter.

- Which plate category applies to your status. *[Verify — categories differ for diplomatic vs. consular vs. administrative/technical staff.]*
- Who lodges the registration, and what documents are required. *[Confirm with your mission.]*
- What changes on transfer of posting or sale of the vehicle. *[Verify de-registration steps.]*

## Tax and customs relief

Relief from certain taxes and duties on vehicles is a recognised feature of diplomatic life in many host states, grounded in the privileges regime, but in France it is **conditional**: on your category of accreditation, on the vehicle, and often on minimum holding periods before resale without losing the relief.

> Do not assume relief, and do not commit to a purchase or import on the assumption of it. Get the current position in writing from your mission and, where relevant, French customs (*la douane*). *[All specifics to verify.]*

## Insurance

Third-party motor insurance is mandatory in France. Diplomatic status does not remove the obligation to be insured.

- Which insurers your mission community uses for diplomatic-plated vehicles. *[Confirm locally.]*
- How a foreign no-claims history is recognised, if at all. *[Verify with insurer.]*
- Cover for dependants who will drive.

## Driving licences

Whether your existing licence is valid for driving in France, for how long, and whether exchange is required, depends on the issuing country and your status.

- Validity of your current licence in France and any exchange requirement. *[Verify — depends on issuing state and reciprocity.]*
- International Driving Permit, if relevant.

## Practical notes

- **Parking** in central Paris is scarce; residential permits and private spaces are worth investigating early.
- **Low-emission rules** apply in and around Paris and affect which vehicles may circulate. *[Verify current zone rules and any exemptions.]*
- **Fuel, tolls and servicing** are ordinary costs and not affected by status.

The honest summary: the privileges regime can make a vehicle more economical for eligible staff, but every relief is conditional and timed. Confirm the current rules for your exact status before you import or buy — and weigh whether you need a car in Paris at all.
