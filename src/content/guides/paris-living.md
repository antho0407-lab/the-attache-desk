Paris hosts bilateral embassies alongside large multilateral communities at the OECD and UNESCO, and the practical experience of arriving differs depending on which you join. This guide covers the ground common to most arrivals: how accreditation and residence work, where the first decisions sit, and the rhythm of the early weeks. It is orientation, not authority — your mission's protocol officer is the person who confirms each step for your case.

## Accreditation and your status

Diplomatic privileges and immunities derive from the **Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (1961)**; consular ones from the **Vienna Convention on Consular Relations (1963)**. In France, accreditation runs through the Protocol department of the Ministry for Europe and Foreign Affairs (*Ministère de l'Europe et des Affaires étrangères*), normally via your mission rather than directly by you.

The document that matters day to day is the **special residence card** issued to accredited staff and dependants (the *titre de séjour spécial*). It evidences your status to French authorities, banks and landlords.

- Who in your mission lodges the accreditation request, and on what timeline? *[Confirm with your mission's protocol officer.]*
- Which dependants are covered, and what each needs to be accredited? *[Verify against current Protocol guidance.]*
- What you carry day to day until the special card is issued. *[Confirm interim arrangements.]*

## Housing

Some missions provide or arrange housing; others give an allowance and leave the search to you. French residential leases, deposits and agency fees follow national rules, but diplomatic tenants sometimes sign under specific arrangements.

> Before signing anything, ask your mission whether leases are signed in your name or the mission's, and how diplomatic status affects the deposit and notice terms. *[Verify — varies by mission and landlord.]*

Practical questions worth settling early: proximity to your chancery or duty station, school catchment if you have children (see the [Schools guide](/guides-by-city/paris/schools)), and whether you will run a vehicle (see [Vehicles](/guides-by-city/paris/vehicles)).

## Banking, utilities and the administrative first weeks

Opening a French bank account, arranging utilities and registering for local services each tend to require proof of address and proof of status. The special residence card and a mission letter usually carry weight here, but requirements vary by provider.

- Bank account: which banks your mission community uses, and what they accept as proof of status. *[Confirm locally.]*
- Health cover: whether you rely on your sending state's scheme, a mission policy, or local arrangements. *[Verify entitlement — do not assume.]*
- Mobile and internet: contract versus prepaid, and what ID is needed.

## Getting around

Paris is dense and well-served by metro, bus, tram and suburban rail, and many postings function comfortably without a private car. If you do bring or buy a vehicle, the diplomatic registration and tax position is a separate topic covered in the [Vehicles guide](/guides-by-city/paris/vehicles).

## A sensible order of operations

1. Confirm accreditation steps and timeline with your protocol officer.
2. Secure housing and a registered address.
3. Open banking and arrange health cover.
4. Settle schooling, if relevant.
5. Decide the vehicle question and register transport.

Treat every figure, threshold and procedure here as something to confirm against current official guidance. The point of this guide is to tell you *what to check* and *in what order* — not to stand in for the authorities who decide your case.
