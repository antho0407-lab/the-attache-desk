Geneva is small for a city of its global weight, and that shapes everything: a concentrated international community, limited and expensive housing, and a daily life that spills across the French border. Many postings here are to the UN Office at Geneva, the WTO, the WHO or a permanent mission, and the practical setup differs from a bilateral embassy. This guide covers the common ground; your mission and the relevant Swiss authorities confirm the specifics.

## Status and registration

For staff of international organisations and permanent missions, Switzerland issues identity documents through the relevant federal authority rather than the ordinary cantonal residence route. The legitimacy card issued to international staff and accredited personnel is the document that evidences your status.

- Which Swiss authority issues your card, and whether your organisation or mission lodges the request. *[Confirm with your HR or protocol officer.]*
- Which dependants are covered and what each needs. *[Verify against current guidance.]*
- What you carry until the card is issued. *[Confirm interim arrangements.]*

Privileges and immunities for mission staff rest on the **Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (1961)**; for international-organisation staff they rest on **headquarters agreements** between Switzerland and the organisation. The two regimes are not identical — know which applies to you.

## The cross-border reality

Geneva's housing is limited and costly, and a large share of the workforce lives across the border in neighbouring France. That is a genuine option for many international staff, but it carries its own administrative and tax consequences.

> Living in France while working in Geneva can affect taxation, social security, healthcare and your card. Do not treat it as a simple housing decision. *[Verify the full cross-border position before committing — it is consequential.]*

## Banking, health cover and the first weeks

- Banking: what local banks accept as proof of status, and which the international community uses. *[Confirm locally.]*
- Health insurance: Switzerland's rules on mandatory health cover, and how they apply to your status and to cross-border residents, are specific and important. *[Verify your obligation and any exemption — do not assume.]*
- Schooling, if relevant, and whether it influences where you settle.

## Getting around

Geneva is compact and well-served by tram, bus and rail, with cross-border public transport into France. Many staff manage without a car. If you do drive, registration and any tax position attached to your status is a separate question to confirm with your mission and the Swiss authorities.

## A sensible order of operations

1. Confirm which status regime and card applies to you.
2. Decide in-canton versus cross-border, with the tax and health consequences understood.
3. Secure housing and a registered address.
4. Arrange banking and confirm your health-cover obligation.
5. Settle schooling, if relevant.

Switzerland's rules — especially on health insurance and cross-border status — are precise and enforced. Treat the specifics here as prompts to verify with your organisation and the federal authorities, not as settled answers.
