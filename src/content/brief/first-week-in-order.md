The instinct on arrival is to do everything at once. Resist it. Most of what makes the first weeks of a posting stressful is not the volume of tasks but their dependencies — doing things in the wrong order and then redoing them.

A defensible sequence holds across most postings. **Status first:** confirm exactly what your mission is lodging on your behalf, on what timeline, and what you carry in the meantime. Almost everything else — a bank account, a lease, a school place — leans on proof of status, so it pays to know precisely where that stands.

**Address second.** A registered address unlocks the administrative layer, and in some cities the choice of neighbourhood is downstream of something else entirely — school availability, your duty station, whether you will run a car. Settle those constraints before you sign a lease, not after.

**Then the administrative spine:** banking, health cover, utilities, connectivity. Each tends to want proof of status and proof of address, which is why they come third, not first.

The unglamorous discipline is writing down who confirmed what. Status, entitlements and procedures vary by mission and change over time, and the person who can answer authoritatively is usually your protocol or HR officer — not a forum, and not a previous posting's memory of how it worked somewhere else.

Speed feels productive. Sequence is what actually gets you settled.
