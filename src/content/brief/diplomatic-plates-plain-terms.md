Special diplomatic registration is one of the more visible markers of status, and one of the most misunderstood. It is worth being clear about what it does and does not mean before a posting turns into a vehicle purchase.

What it signals is straightforward: that a vehicle belongs to an accredited mission or eligible staff member, registered through a special process rather than the ordinary one. The categories differ — diplomatic, consular, administrative and technical staff are not always treated the same — and the host state, not the driver, defines them.

What it does **not** mean is exemption from the basics. Mandatory insurance is still mandatory. Traffic rules still apply. And the tax and customs relief that often accompanies eligible vehicles is conditional, not automatic: it can depend on your category of accreditation, on the vehicle, and frequently on a minimum holding period before you can resell without losing the benefit.

That last point is where people get caught. The relief can make a vehicle genuinely more economical — and then a transfer of posting arrives before the holding period is up, and the sums change.

The honest guidance is dull but reliable: don't commit to importing or buying on the assumption of relief. Get the current position for your exact status in writing from your mission and, where relevant, the host customs authority — and weigh, separately, whether you need a car in the city at all. In a place with good transport, the simplest answer is often no vehicle.
